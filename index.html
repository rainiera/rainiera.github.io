I study computer science at UT Austin. In my free time, I read weird blogs and experiment with strategies to live and think better.

Interests (last updated 1/22/18):

As a filter, each of these are topics I usually spend at least 10 hours a week thinking about. They overlap sometimes.

  • Startups. I like thinking about business models and making predictions about which startups and technology trends will fail or succeed, especially with my friends. I’ve also leaned towards working at startups (historically of the 10, 20, 100, and 1000-person size). One of my favorite publications to improve this skill and update my mental models/probabilities is Stratechery. I’m also a 1517 Fund Grant recipient and continue to advise and consult student startups.
  • Linguistics. History of language and natural language processing. I definitely recommend Bill Bryson’s The Mother Tongue - English And How It Got That Way for the former and Stanford’s CS224n: Natural Language Processing with Deep Learning for the latter.
  • Forecasting. I’m interested in finding out what certain people—particularly successful founders or VCs—see that I (and most others) can’t see. I’m conscious of the fact that I’m usually an optimistic, positive person, so much that it might actually make me poor at probabilistic thinking. My take is that this places an irrational prior on the probability I place on a prediction if its upside/utility (if applicable) is high. (Example: As a thought experiment, I’ve asked friends at which age they would choose to die if it was guaranteed that they would die at this age and could not die earlier. I think the median answer is around 80, but I’ve said 200 because I’m bullish on human longevity and improvements to keeping our brains sharp.) On the other hand, this optimism makes me less risk-averse and possibly more likely to make decisions that have this high upside (but others may view as silly). Listen to Phil Tetlock on “Superforecasting: The Art and Science of Prediction” on the Rationally Speaking podcast and read The Signal and the Noise: Why So Many Predictions Fail—but Some Don’t by Nate Silver if you want to learn more about this topic.
  • Rationality and behavioral economics. I think things such as cognitive biases are super neat. My interest in this topic sparked by reading Thinking, Fast and Slow by Daniel Kahneman when I started college, but I hadn’t thought deeply about it again until last year, when I read Algorithms to Live By by Brian Christian and Tom Griffiths, and began listening to the Rationally Speaking podcast, of which I am a huge fan.
  • Data-intensive applications. I’ve developed Python, Java, and Go code for production environments that have handled high traffic or large batches of data at a time.
  • Books and podcasts. I read a lot and listen to podcasts while cooking and doing chores. If you are in tech and looking to get into podcasts, my first was Gimlet Media’s Startup, where in the first season, the founder of Gimlet Media records his media company’s founding from his perspective, including an entertainingly bad pitch to a VC and the struggles of starting a company. Many of Gimlet’s podcasts contain great storytelling, especially one of my favorites, Long Distance from Reply All. But in addition to these, I recommend NPR’s Planet Money, which explains the economy in an accessible way and Strong Towns, which is awesome.
  • People management, mentorship, and organizational strategy. I am a huge proponent of the sayings, “Be who you needed when you were younger” and “Meetings are for laughter” (which I may have made up?). I live these maxims out by directing Convergent’s incubator and product development curriculum, plus attempting to follow the Socratic method whenever I get the chance to teach.
  • Blockchain technology. Interestingly, this is the best anti-blockchain article I’ve read recently: Ten years in, nobody has come up with a use for blockchain. And the best anti-cryptocurrency article I’ve read recently: What Bitcoin Shows Us About How Money Works.
